Abstract
Bilateral filter (BF) theory is applied to integrate spatial contextual information into the spectral domain for improving the accuracy of the support vector machine (SVM) classifier. The proposed classification framework is a two-stage process. First, an edge-preserved smoothing is carried out on a hyperspectral image (HSI). Then, the SVM multiclass classifier is applied on the smoothed HSI. One of the advantages of the BF-based implementation is that it considers the spatial as well as spectral closeness for smoothing the HSI. Therefore, the proposed method provides better smoothing in the homogeneous region and preserves the image details, which in turn improves the separability between the classes. The performance of the proposed method is tested using benchmark HSIs obtained from the airborne-visible-infrared-imaging-spectrometer (AVIRIS) and the reflective-optics-system-imaging-spectrometer (ROSIS) sensors. Experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the edge-preserved filtering in the classification of the HSI. Average accuracies (with 10% training samples) of the proposed classification framework are 99.04%, 98.11%, and 96.42% for AVIRIS–Salinas, ROSIS–Pavia University, and AVIRIS–Indian Pines images, respectively. Since the proposed method follows a combination of BF and the SVM formulations, it will be quite simple and practical to implement in real applications.

1.

Introduction

A hyperspectral image (HSI) can provide useful information regarding the physical nature of the different target materials, which can be utilized to distinguish objects in the image. A chain of processing algorithms is usually applied to retrieve information from the HSI. Classification is one of these algorithms that assign a label to a pixel in the HSI, which in turn can be used for developing thematic maps. In HSI classification, the number of training samples available for some of the classes may be less than the number of HSI bands. Therefore, the reliable estimation of statistical parameters becomes difficult due to the small number of training samples and the large number of features.1 Moreover, the spatial variability due to noise may also degrade the accuracy of the classification algorithms.23.4.–5

Remotely sensed HSIs consist of heterogeneous classes for which only a few training samples are available. Therefore, considering the geometrical properties (geometrical margin between the classes) rather than the statistical properties of the classes is crucial in HSI classification task. The support vector machine (SVM) is one of the widely used pixelwise classifiers that utilizes the geometrical properties of the HSI data and provides good classification accuracy in fewer training sets. SVM utilizes the concept of geometrical margin maximization, and it does not require the statistical distributions of classes to perform the classification task. In other words, SVM constructs a hyperplane or set of hyperplanes in a high-dimensional space, which can be used for classification of the HSI. Moreover, the literature reveals that the accuracy of the SVM can be further improved by incorporating the spatial contextual information (information of spatial relationship between pixels within a neighborhood) into the kernel function of the SVM classifier.6,7 Plaza et al.6 have proposed a simple feature extraction using moment criteria for improving the performance of the SVM classifier. They have considered the mean and standard deviation of the neighborhood pixels in a spatial window per spectral channel for redefining the pixels. However, the moment-based method cannot incorporate the geometric closeness and the spectral similarity of the pixels within a neighborhood. Moreover, they have not considered improving the signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) of the HSI before applying the classifier. In specific, a weighted average smoothing function that considers the geometric closeness and the spectral similarity between pixels may be a better choice to redefine the pixels based on the spatial information. Moreover, the weighted average smoothing may preserve the image details while removing the noise, which in turn improves the SNR of the HSI. Hence, a preprocessing method that integrates spatial contextual information into the spectral domain and reduces the spatial–spectral variability (due to noise) may be a better candidate to improve the accuracy of the classifier.

It is interesting to note that bilateral filter (BF) theory8 is well suited for the abovementioned formulation. Further, the standard BF is a nonlinear filter that smooths the image by performing the weighted averaging of the neighborhood pixels (considering the geometric and photometric closeness between pixels). Therefore, it has the capability to preserve the image details while smoothing out the spatial variability due to noise. Since the BF filter is a weighted averaging filter, it may also be applied as a preprocessing step to integrate the spatial information into the spectral domain. Keeping such a view, we propose a classification framework that employs the BF theory to integrate the spatial contextual information into the spectral domain for improving the accuracy of the SVM classifier. Further, the range weight estimation of the filter is modified based on the spectral similarity within a neighborhood. We also study the influence of the BF parameters on the classification accuracy. The performance of the proposed framework is tested on three real HSIs obtained from airborne-visible-infrared-imaging-spectrometer (AVIRIS) and reflective-optics-system-imaging-spectrometer (ROSIS) sensors. Results are compared with other spatial/spectral smoothing methods. Results are also compared with a baseline method that employs an edge-preserved filter as a postprocessing method.9 In addition to this, we have examined the influence of the proposed preprocessing scheme on different SVM kernels [linear kernel, radial-basis-function (RBF) kernel, and polynomial kernel]. Experiment results demonstrate that the proposed preprocessing approach can significantly improve the classification accuracy of SVM.

2.

Problem Formulation

A supervised HSI classification problem can be formulated as follows. Let S≡{1,2,…,i} denote the set of pixels, I=(I→1,I→2,…,I→i)∈Rk×i be the k-dimensional vectors representing reflectance spectra in the image, L≡{1,2,…,i} be the set of class labels, and c=(c1,c2,…,ci) be the classification map of the labels. The goal of classification tasks is to assign a label ci to each pixel in the HSI.

Among all the pixel-based classifiers, the SVM classifier has in particular shown a good performance regarding classification accuracy.10 SVM is one of the widely used pixelwise classifiers that utilize the geometrical properties rather than the statistical properties of the HSI data. As a two-class classifier, the SVM projects the input training set into a Hilbert space H by some mapping function Φ, where it is expected to find a linear separation that maximizes the geometrical margin between two classes. Thus, SVM is able to fully exploit the discrimination capability of the relatively few training samples available. The optimal hyperplane can be found by maximizing the margin and minimizing the sum of errors, ∑i=1nξi. This particular problem is formulated as a dual optimization problem, and this is solved using Lagrange multipliers,

(1)

maxα∑i=1nαi−12∑i,j=1nαiαjyiyj〈ϕ(xi),ϕ(xj)〉H,
subject to 0<αi<C, ∑i=1nαiyi=0, where xi and xj are feature vectors and α is a Lagrange multiplier.

The inner product in H can be computed implicitly using kernel functions K.11 In SVM, kernel mapping will happen based on the inner product. Hence, the kernel function can be defined as

(2)

K(xi,xj)=〈ϕ(xi)·ϕ(xj)〉.
Popular kernel functions are linear kernel (SVML) K(xi,xj)=xi·xj, polynomial kernel (SVMP) K(xi,xj)=(xi·xj+1)d, d∈Z+, and RBF kernel (SVMR) K(xi,xj)=exp(‖xi−xj‖2/2γ2), γ∈R.

In the HSI classification context, it is possible to improve the accuracy of the SVM classifier by including the spatial contextual information into the kernel.6,7 The spatial contextual information can be incorporated into the SVM classifier by redefining the HSI pixel based on the spatial–spectral information. The simplest spatial features that can be extracted from a given region are based on moment criteria.6 However, the moment-based method cannot incorporate the geometric closeness and the spectral similarity of the pixels within a neighborhood. Moreover, the moment-based method (mean or standard deviation) is a feature extraction method, which cannot be used for improving the SNR of the HSI. In specific, a weighted average smoothing function that considers the geometric closeness and the spectral similarity between pixels may be a better choice to redefine the pixels based on the spatial information.

However, preservation of spatial image details (e.g., edges) is very important while redefining each HSI pixel based on the weighted averaging of the neighborhood pixels. That means, to integrate the spatial contextual information without affecting the image details, the following criteria need to be considered. (1) The weighted averaging of the pixels should take care for the geometric closeness and the spectral similarity of the pixels within the spatial window. (2) The method should preserve the spatial and spectral details (e.g., edges). Based on these criteria, the weighted averaging of the pixels (within a band) in a spatial neighborhood can be formulated as

(3)

F[Ip]=∑q∈SW[p,q]·Iq∑q∈SW[p,q],
where Ip denotes the pixel intensity at position p, F[·] designates the weighted averaging function, S is a set of all possible image locations (spatial domain), and ∑q∈S denotes a sum over all image pixels indexed by q (neighborhood pixels). W[p,q] is the weight factor, which can be computed based on the content of the neighborhood. To preserve the image details, the weight factor should be computed based on the geometric closeness and the amount of pixel similarity within a neighborhood. Hence, the W[p,q] can be defined as the multiplication of WG[p,q] and WR[p,q], where WG measures the weights for the geometric closeness and WR measures the weights for the pixel similarity.

This weighted averaging–based approach can be extended to HSIs by considering each pixel as a spectrum. Let S≡{1,2,…,i} denotes the set of pixels, and let I=(I→1,I→2,…,I→i)∈Rk×i be the k-dimensional vectors representing reflectance spectra in the image. A vector I→i can be defined as I→i={Ii,1,Ii,2,…,Ii,k}; then, the weighted averaging operator on each element can be written as

(4)

F[I→i]={F[Ii,1],F[Ii,2],…,F[Ii,k]},
where Ii,k denotes the i’th pixel in the k’th band of the HSI, which is similar to Ip in Eq. (3). In the HSI classification context, I→i will be the input feature vector (xi=I→i). When we apply the weighted average function on the HSI, the resulting input spectra can be denoted as F[I→i]. Therefore, in Eq. (2), xi becomes F[I→i]. Using this formulation, every input training sample given to the SVM classifier can be redefined, which in turn forms the kernel matrix (K). Then the corresponding kernel matrix can be defined as

(5)

K(xi,xj)=⟨ϕ(xi),ϕ(xj)⟩,

(6)

K(xi,xj)=〈ϕ(F[I→i]),ϕ(F[I→j])〉.
Note that solving the minimization problem for the SVM kernel requires the same number of constraints as in the conventional SVM algorithm. Therefore, additional computations are not required in the proposed approach.

It is interesting to note that BF theory8 satisfies the abovementioned criteria for integrating spatial contextual information into the spectral domain. In BF, the pixels are transformed based on the weighted averaging of the neighborhood pixels (considering the geometric and photometric closeness). The standard BF applied on a grayscale image is defined as8

(7)

BF[I]p=1Wp∑q∈SGσs(‖p−q‖)Gσr(‖Ip−Iq‖)Iq,
where the normalization factor Wp ensures the weights sum to 1.0. The set of all possible image locations is named as the spatial domain (S), and the set of all possible pixel values is named as the range domain (R):

(8)

Wp=∑q∈SGσs(‖p−q‖)Gσr(‖Ip−Iq‖),

(9)

Gσs(‖p−q‖)=12πσs2exp(−‖p−q‖22σs2),

(10)

Gσr(‖Ip−Iq‖)=12πσr2exp(−‖Ip−Iq‖22σr2),
where Gσs is a spatial Gaussian weighting that decreases the influence of distant pixels. Gσr is a range Gaussian weighting that decreases the influence of pixel q when its intensity value differs from Ip (center pixel). Parameters σs and σr represent the standard deviation of the Gaussian function Gσs and Gσr, respectively. ‖·‖ denotes the L2 norm. In terms of filtering, parameters σs (domain filtering) and σr (range filtering) specify the amount of filtering for the image I. A large spatial parameter σs smooths larger features in the image. In homogeneous regions, neighborhood pixels in close proximity are similar to each other. In this case, the BF acts as a standard Gaussian filter and averages away the weak differences between pixels. At sharp boundaries, BF replaces the bright pixel at the center (bright side of the boundary) with the weighted average of the bright pixels in its close proximity, and the dark pixels are ignored because of their negligible weights. BF has been successfully implemented in various applications of computational photography.1213.14.15.16.17.–18 It has also been used for HSI fusion,19 segmentation,20 and postprocessing9 applications.

The BF adopts a low-pass Gaussian filter for both the spatial and range domains. Smoothing usually relies on small spatial kernels, and the range kernels are often chosen to match the noise level. Combining the range filter and the domain filter, a BF at an edge pixel becomes an elongated Gaussian filter that is oriented along the edges.8 This ensures that averaging is done mostly along the edges, and it is substantially reduced in the gradient direction. From the frequency domain perspective, the BF at a pixel is low pass in one direction, and almost all-pass in the orthogonal direction.21 This explains the capability of BF to smooth the image while preserving edge structures.

In a grayscale image, the standard BF [see Eq. (7)] does weighted averaging based on the geometric closeness and the pixel similarities within a neighborhood. In HSI, each pixel represents a reflectance spectrum of the target material. Therefore, spectral similarity can provide useful information about the neighborhood spectra in HSI. Generally, range weights of the BF can be adjusted to any metric that computes the difference between two pixels. For HSIs, range weights can be computed using the whole spectrum information instead of computing the L2 norm between pixels in each band individually. Therefore, the BF for HSI (hereafter referred to as HBF) can be defined as

(11)

HBF[I]p,k=1Wp∑q∈SGσs(‖p−q‖)Gσr(‖I→p−I→q‖)Iq,k,

(12)

Wp=∑q∈SGσs(‖p−q‖)Gσr(‖I→p−I→q‖),
where I→p and I→q are the reflectance spectra of center pixel p and a pixel at a neighboring position q, respectively; k represents an HSI band (k=1,2,…,N):

(13)

Gσs(‖p−q‖)=12πσs2exp(−‖p−q‖22σs2),

(14)

Gσr(‖I→p−I→q‖)=12πσr2exp(−‖I→p−I→q‖22σr2).

Therefore, in the above-mentioned formulation [Eq. (6)], if we replaced the function F[·] with the HBF function (HBF[·]), integration of the spatial contextual information into the spectral domain will become more simple and flexible to implement, that is, a simple weighted average smoothing as a preprocessing step may improve the accuracy of the SVM classifier.

3.

Spatial-Spectral Smoothing with Bilateral Filter

Three standard HSIs captured by the AVIRIS (Indian Pines site and Salinas site) and ROSIS (University of Pavia site) sensors are used in this study. The AVIRIS–Indian Pines image (Fig. 1) was captured from the Indian Pines test site in northwestern Indiana, and the AVIRIS–Salinas image (Fig. 2) was captured from Salinas Valley, California. ROSIS images (Fig. 3) were acquired from the Pavia University test site, northern Italy. Dimensions of the images are 145×145×200 for AVIRIS–Indian Pines, 512×217×200 for AVIRIS–Salinas, and 610×610×103 for the ROSIS image. Ground truth data available for the AVIRIS and the ROSIS images are used for testing the classification performance. Pixel intensities of all the images used in the study are in the range {0,1}.

Figure 4 shows the effect of smoothing on spatial domain. It shows the spatial profile (single row of ROSIS image) view of the across-track direction. Small variations along the across-track direction are smoothed out by applying HBF on the HSI. Large variations or details have been preserved due to the edge-preserved filtering. Figure 5 shows the smoothed spectra obtained by applying HBF on the ROSIS image. These spectra are extracted from a homogeneous region (Meadows in Fig. 2). They show that smoothing of the HSI can reduce the variability among spectra within a homogeneous region without modifying the spectral characteristics. In other words, spectra within a homogeneous region become more similar due to smoothing. Therefore, the reduction in both spatial and spectral variability within each class may improve the interclass separability.

Raw ROSIS spectra and the corresponding HBF-filtered spectra.

In images, smoothing generally reduces the variability in pixels. In HSI, HBF-based smoothing may reduce the variability in spectra in close proximity. If the spectra in a particular class (e.g., tree, grass, and soil) become more similar due to image smoothing, then the classification accuracy may improve significantly. We adopt the Jeffries–Matusita distance (JMD)5 to study the effect of smoothing on interclass separability. JMD is a transformation of the Bhattacharyya distance.22 A practical advantage of this measure is that it tends to suppress high separability while overemphasizing low separability values.5 The key advantage of JMD is that when two classes are totally separated, JMD tends to saturate, and the value becomes the square root of two. In contrast, JMD would become zero for the complete overlapping of two classes:

(15)

JMDij=2[1−exp(−β)],

(16)

β=18[μi−μj]T[Σi+Σj2]−1[μi−μj]+12loge[|Σi|+|Σj|2|Σi||Σj|],
where JMDij is the JMD between two classes i and j, μi and μj are the averages, and Σi and Σj are the covariance matrix for classes i and j, respectively.

Figure 6 shows the trend in interclass separability with respect to the HBF parameter σr. As σr increases, the more smoothing may take place, and finally the variation within a homogeneous region becomes very small. JMD is applied between different classes of AVIRIS images (Fig. 6). In Fig. (6), class-1, class-2, class-3, and class-4 represent the ground-truth features corn-notill, soybean-notill, soybean-mintill, and corn, respectively.

Interclass separability (JMD) with respect to the HBF range parameter. Class-1, class-2, class-3, and class-4 represent the AVIRIS image ground-truth features corn-notill, soybean-notill, soybean-mintill, and corn, respectively.

4.

Classification of Smoothed Hyperspectral Image

We consider a SVM multiclass classifier with linear kernel (SVML), RBF kernel (SVMR), and polynomial kernel (SVMP) to test the performance of the proposed framework. In our approach, classification consists of two steps: (1) apply the HBF on the HSI (raw images); (2) do the SVM-based23 classification on the filtered images. Classification is performed on the AVIRIS–Indian Pines, AVIRIS–Salinas, and ROSIS dataset. Pixel-vectors in test HSIs are divided randomly into training (30%) and testing sets (70%). Training samples are mean centered and scaled, and the testing samples are also mean centered and scaled using the mean and the standard deviation obtained from the training samples. In the context of HSI classification, some pioneering studies show the effectiveness of SVM to classify the HSI directly in the hyper-dimensional feature space.2425.–26 Therefore, in this study feature selection is not considered for the classification of HSI. We follow a one-against-one multiclass classification strategy for the SVM classifier. SVM kernel parameters are tuned iteratively for obtaining good classification results. The RBF kernel width, the polynomial order (d), and regularization parameter (C) are tuned for the SVM. The Gaussian kernel is tuned for the range of (.01,…,1), d in the range of (1,…,10), and C in the range of (101,…,103). A one-against-one multiclassification scheme was adopted in this study. Two widely used indexes, namely, the overall accuracy (OA) and the average accuracy (AA), are adopted to evaluate the performance of the proposed classification framework. OA is the percentage of correctly classified pixels. AA is the mean of the percentage of correctly classified pixels for each class.

Studies have already reported that the performance of the classification algorithm may be adversely affected due to the low SNR of the image.3,4,27 Therefore, we also use other spatial/spectral smoothing methods as preprocessing methods to examine their influence on the classifier accuracy. We compare the performance of the proposed method with other smoothing methods such as multiple linear regression (MLR),28 total-variation (TV) smoothing,29 and spatial-spectral TV (SSTV) smoothing.30 MLR is applied as a spectral smoothing method. The TV algorithm is employed to achieve spatial smoothing, and SSTV is used as a spatial–spectral smoothing method. The TV-based spatial smoothing method is applied to compare the performance, mainly due to its similarity with the BF approach.31 In the TV method, the total variation-based penalty is employed to compute the filter weights instead of the exponential terms used in the standard BF.8 To compare the performance of the proposed method to other smoothing methods and to evaluate their performance on a limited number of samples, only 10% of samples (randomly selected) are considered for training, and the remaining 90% of samples are considered for testing the classifier.

5.

Results and Discussions

The strength of the HBF lies in its simplicity and flexibility.32 The filter weights can be adjusted to any metric that computes the difference between two pixels. In HSI, smoothing based on the HBF can decrease the spatial (Fig. 4) as well as the spectral variability (Fig. 5) within a small geographical setting. That means spectra within a homogeneous region become more similar due to smoothing, which in turn improves the interclass separability and the classification accuracy. Selection of the filter parameter is crucial in HBF-based smoothing. Figure 7 shows the map that demonstrates the variations in the classification accuracy based on the range parameter σr and the spatial parameter σs. It shows that the maximum classification accuracy can be possible with σs=4, and there should be a minimum σr value to get better results. This can be attributed to the fact that pixels with values much closer to each other than σr are mixed together and values much more distant than σr are not considered for the smoothing of the center pixel. That means the neighborhood pixels with distance less than σr are considered to smooth the center pixel, which in turn can reduce the spatial as well as spectral variability within a spatial window. If the intensity of the image is amplified or attenuated, σr must be adjusted accordingly in order to keep the same smoothing results. In this study, the image intensities are kept within the range {0,1}. Therefore, we can generalize this result for all the HSIs within this range. In HBF, the kernel size should be greater than σs. If the kernel size of the HBF is too small, it will not be able to capture most of the pixel variations. If the kernel size is too large, it might be difficult to weight pixels based on the spatial variations. To find the optimum kernel size, we kept the range parameter (σr=0.5) and spatial parameter (σs=4) constant and varied the kernel size for finding the corresponding variation in classification accuracy. For all the test images, a 9×9 kernel gives better results regarding computation time and overall classification accuracy.

Fig. 7

Map showing the variation in the classification accuracy based on the spatial parameter and the range parameter. (a) AVIRIS image and (b) ROSIS image.

The SVM classifier maps the input feature space into a higher dimensional space by a kernel transformation, where it is expected to find a linear separation that maximizes the geometrical margin between the two classes. Thus, SVM can fully exploit the discrimination capability of the relatively few training samples available. But the large spectral variability of each information class may negatively impact its generalization capability. Therefore, the effective solution to this problem due to noise is to reduce the spatial and spectral variability in the data. In this context, HBF may act as a preprocessing method to improve the generalization capability of the SVM classifier. The advantage of the HBF is that it considers the spectral closeness within a spatial window for smoothing the HSI. Therefore, the spectral variation within proximity may reduce, which in turn may reduce the variability within a class significantly, and this helps the SVM to accurately classify the pixel-vectors (or spectra). Tables 1 and 2 show the classwise results of different methods applied in this study. All the three SVM algorithms have improved their accuracy by adopting HBF as a preprocessing technique. Figures 8Fig. 9–10 show the classification map obtained from the SVM classifier implemented on raw images and the HBF-filtered images. It clearly demonstrates that the SVM applied to raw images has errors distributed across all the classes; specifically, it has errors at classes with fewer samples. However, the classification on the HBF-filtered image shows minimum errors in the spatially similar region (homogeneous area), and the errors only appear near to the class boundaries. This may be due to the spectral artifacts that occurred due to the HBF filtering at the edges in the image. This artifact may be reduced by applying spatially adaptive edge-preserved HBF.

Optimum parameters are tuned iteratively for different kernels. For SVMR, parameters are obtained as C=60 and Gaussian kernel width as 0.02. For SVMP, parameters are d=4 and C=30. Performance of the proposed framework is tested with AVIRIS–Indian Pines, AVIRIS–Salinas, and ROSIS images (see Tables 1Table 2–3) and shows the performance of the SVM classifier on raw images and the corresponding filtered images. When smoothed images are given as inputs to the classifier, the accuracy is improved significantly. The proposed method gives the best performance regarding average accuracy. Each experiment (with 30% training samples) was repeated 10 times, and the mean accuracy values are reported to evaluate the robustness of the SVM on the smoothed images. Table 4 shows the mean accuracy and the corresponding standard deviation obtained by varying the training and testing samples in the image. Standard deviations of the OA are much less for the HBF-filtered images. This result shows the robustness of the proposed framework to the sample variations. This is explained by the fact that the classes with a large number of error samples are smoothed out and they become similar to their neighborhood spectra. Hence, the reduction in the spatial variability makes it easier to apply the regularization mechanism implemented in the SVM formulation, resulting in significant robustness to the sample variations.

In this study, the HBF was applied as a method to incorporate the spatial contextual information into the spectral domain. In other respect, the HBF can be considered as a spatial–spectral smoothing method for improving the SNR of the HSI. In this regard, other smoothing methods may also provide good result in the classification accuracy. Therefore, we examine the potential of other smoothing methods for improving the accuracy of the SVM classifier. Since the RBF kernel gives very good results for all the test cases, we select SVMR for comparing the performance of different smoothing methods. To evaluate the performance on a limited number of samples, only 10% of samples are considered for training the classifier, and the remaining 90% of samples are used for testing the classifier. Results are shown in Table 5. It can be seen that all the smoothing methods show reasonable increment (compared to the raw image) in the OA and AA. The reason is that the different smoothing methods may improve the SNR of the HSI, which in turn reduces the spatial variability in the data. The MLR method shows poor performance among other methods due to the smoothing performed only in the spectral domain. Among other total variation methods, the SSTV method displays a reasonable increment in the OA for all the test cases. This performance may be due to the simultaneous smoothing of the spatial and the spectral domains. However, among all the smoothing methods, the proposed method displays excellent classification accuracy (see Table 5). TV methods automatically adjust the smoothing strength based on the noise content of the image. Therefore, total variation methods are useful for reducing the noise, but they will not provide a significant amount of smoothing that is required for reducing the spectral variability in each class. In contrast, HBF replaces the center pixel in a spatial window with the weighted average value of the neighborhood pixels while considering the geometric and the spectral similarity within a neighborhood. Consequently, they provide weighted average smoothing while preserving the image details, and the smoothing strength can be easily controlled by adjusting the range parameter of the filter. Due to this, the HBF can be used as an edge-preserved smoothing filter to reduce the spatial–spectral variability, which in turn reduces the spectral variability within each class and significantly increases the separability between two classes.

Figure 11 shows the absolute difference between the actual ground-truth map and the classified map. Locations of the classification errors are shown as white dots in this figure. Since MLR performs spectral smoothing, the amount of spatial smoothing achieved by MLR will be less. Similarly, the TV method provides smoothing only for the spatial domain without considering the spectral domain. Therefore, MLR and TV methods produce more misclassified pixels in homogeneous regions and class boundaries (see Table 4 for the corresponding AA). Figure 11 also reveals that both MLR and TV methods fail to produce accurate results. However, the proposed method produces fewer misclassified pixels in homogeneous regions and the class boundaries due to the edge-preserved smoothing. Our method also produces the difference image with minimum misclassified pixels for the classes with fewer samples, which in turn keeps AA comparable to OA (see Table 4). This result further confirms the robustness of the proposed classification framework to spatial variations. Results of the proposed method are also compared with one of the recent studies9 that applied the SVM as a classifier and the BF as a postprocessing technique. Using 10% training samples, our method (OA=96.50%) outperformed the baseline method (OA=95.10%) for AVIRIS–Indian Pines data. Using 2% training samples, our method (OA=96.91%) outperformed the baseline method (OA=96.2%) for AVIRIS–Salinas data. Using 2% training samples, our method (OA=97.17%) outperformed the baseline method (OA=96.30%) for ROSIS data. All the above results suggest that HBF can be an effective and simple preprocessing technique to improve the classification of the HSIs.

Since the filter follows a center pixel dominance approach, computational time per pixel is a function of the (spatial) filter size r. Therefore, a brute-force implementation for BF can calculate each output pixel in O(r2) time. Several acceleration schemes have already been proposed33 to reduce the computation time of the standard BF. Further improvement in the acceleration has already been achieved by mapping the algorithm onto modern graphics hardware, obtaining computation times in the order of a few milliseconds. Therefore, HBF may be implemented for real-time HSI applications.

6.

Conclusion

The proposed method aims at improving the SVM classification accuracy by applying an edge-preserved filter as a preprocessing method. The proposed framework consists of the following two steps. First, the HBF is applied for obtaining spatial–spectral smoothing in HSI. Then the SVM classifier is applied on the smoothed image. SVM classifier with linear, RBF, and polynomial kernels has shown good performance on the smoothed images. In this study, the HBF has been applied as a simple preprocessing method. But the accuracy of the SVM classifier is improved significantly. This paper shows that a proper preprocessing approach is able to achieve a high classification accuracy, namely, 99.15% for the AVIRIS–Salinas, 99.01% for the ROSIS, and 98.49% for the AVIRIS–Indian Pines image. Moreover, with a limited number of training samples (10%) the proposed method gives very good results, that is, AVIRIS–Indian Pines (OA=96.50%), AVIRIS–Salinas (OA=98.56%), and ROSIS (OA=98.52%). In the future, further improvement may be achieved by adaptively estimating the filter parameters. In this study, we have not explored the possibilities of using any feature selection approaches for improving the performance of the classifier. The improvement in classification accuracy using feature selection approaches combined with the preprocessing method could be a topic for future investigation.
